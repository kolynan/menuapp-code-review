## Questions Analyzed

### Q1: Logging race conditions in `ThreadPoolExecutor`
**Recommendation:** Use **Variant B** by default: one global logger with `%(threadName)s` in the formatter. Reserve **Variant A** for the narrower case where each worker thread truly needs its own persistent context or sink configuration.

**Reasoning:** The standard `logging` stack is already thread-safe, and `%(threadName)s` gives you per-thread attribution without multiplying logger instances or handler setup. In a pooled executor, thread-local state can outlive one task and accidentally leak context into the next task that reuses the same worker thread. A global logger also makes filtering, aggregation, and structured export simpler when you are diagnosing concurrency issues across multiple modules. Variant A is justified only when you need thread-scoped metadata that is awkward to inject any other way, or different handlers per long-lived worker.

**Trade-offs:** Variant B is less flexible if you want extra per-task fields beyond the thread name, so you may still need `LoggerAdapter`, `extra`, or a correlation ID. Variant A offers stronger local isolation, but it is easier to misconfigure, easier to forget to clear, and harder to keep consistent across the codebase. In `ThreadPoolExecutor`, the biggest pitfall is assuming thread-local equals task-local; it does not.

**Mobile UX:** This is not directly user-facing, but simpler and more reliable logs shorten incident triage during lunch and dinner peaks. In a restaurant flow, faster diagnosis matters because a race can surface as delayed orders, duplicate notifications, or stale waiter state.
